RODS (0.84) is the standout: the Popoviciu bound connection (GRPO gradient mass concentrates on highest reward-variance samples → samples at the capability boundary) gives a theoretically grounded zero-cost boundary detector that directly informs when and what to synthesize. This closes a loop that PathRouter (Jun 17) opened from the retrieval side: both papers argue that the *structure* of what gets trained on (dependency topology for tool chains, boundary proximity for data synthesis) determines RL efficiency more than total data volume.

Preference Instability in Reward Models (0.83) provides the mechanistic complement to HARVE (Jun 8) and PRIME (Jun 17): HARVE edits reward-head vectors to suppress hacking representations, PRIME tracks the policy's internalized hacking capability, and this paper identifies and suppresses the brittle surface-feature shortcuts that cause RM preferences to flip under semantic-preserving perturbations — a *pre-hacking* robustness intervention rather than a post-hacking correction.

HiMPO (0.80) is the most novel extension of the credit assignment thread: HiPER/AT-RL/R3L/RReDCoT all addressed credit for reasoning steps; HiMPO addresses credit specifically for memory-writing actions, where the causal chain is longer and tool/reasoning errors in between create spurious credit entanglement. The hindsight local utility estimator (comparing recoverable task-relevant information before/after the write) is a clean solution to the counterfactual credit problem for memory.

EnvRL (0.79) adds world-model-style environment dynamics learning as auxiliary GRPO objectives (state prediction + inverse dynamics) without extra data. The complementarity with InfoMem (Jun 10) is instructive: InfoMem gave the memory module a denser reward signal; EnvRL gives the action policy a richer environment model — both address the sparse reward problem for long-horizon agents from different angles.

ViGOS (0.79) completes a three-paper arc on multimodal interference: AT-RL (Jun 14, token-level credit attribution), factorized reward (Jun 17, per-modality reward segments), ViGOS (Jun 18, teacher decoupling at supervision level). Three orthogonal solutions to cross-modal interference, suggesting the problem is genuinely multi-faceted.

# From Reasoning Traces to Reusable Modules: Understanding Compositional Generalization in Language Model Reasoning

## Summary

A hierarchical latent selection model formalizes why SFT+RL achieves compositional generalization: SFT supplies atomic modules (skills and routing operations) in compound reasoning traces, while RL decomposes those traces to identify and recombine the latent modules for novel configurations. Controlled experiments confirm that RL can extract modules from SFT-provided compound traces and apply them to new task compositions outside the SFT support, with compound trace training outperforming isolated atomic module training.

## Key Contributions

- Hierarchical latent selection model: reasoning traces generated by composing discrete latent selection variables (skills = local ops, routing = how intermediate info is selected/composed)
- Theoretical proof that SFT and RL play asymmetric, complementary roles: SFT covers atomic modules, RL identifies and enables compositional generalization
- Empirical finding: training on compound traces generalizes better than training on isolated atomic modules
- Practical protocol: SFT should ensure coverage of all atomic modules via compositional traces; RL should focus on novel compositions outside SFT support

## Relevance

This is the theoretical backbone paper for understanding why SFT→RL post-training works at all, directly connecting to the Select-and-Improve mechanistic decomposition (Jun 12) and the ANCORA self-play paper (Jun 15): where Select-and-Improve observed that SFT provides diversity and RL provides improvement, this paper explains *why* at the latent-module level. The asymmetric roles finding also clarifies what GRPO variants (DiScO, S2L-PO, ANCORA) are doing when they vary the rollout source.

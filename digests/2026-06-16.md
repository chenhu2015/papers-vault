**All 5 digest papers from queries 2 and 3.** ExpRL (0.85) is the standout: the "references as reward scaffolds" insight cleanly separates the *information value* of expert solutions (for constructing grading rubrics) from their *imitation cost* (distributional drift that GAPD and Quality-Utility Paradox both show is harmful). The LLM judge then bridges the RLHF and agentic RL threads: it provides the kind of process-level dense reward that PRMs give for reasoning chains, but without PRM training data — instead grounding rubric generation in the reference itself. First-Principles Survey (0.79) is the synthesis paper the past six weeks have been building toward: the trajectory/reward two-axis framework complements the Credit Assignment Survey (Jun 14) taxonomically, and the compound failures category is the new research frontier it identifies — the class of problems no single-axis fix resolves. S2L-PO (0.76) resolves a clean empirical puzzle: if smaller models have *lower mean accuracy* but *higher diversity*, they are better explorers than the learner itself for GRPO rollout generation — three existing diversity mechanisms (DiScO, ANCORA, S2L-PO) now form a complete picture of where rollout diversity comes from: problem distribution (ANCORA), reward signal (DiScO), source model (S2L-PO). StepOPSD (0.76) connects credit assignment (step granularity like R3L's Pivotal Credit) to distillation (hindsight-enriched teacher rescoring) without requiring external labels, completing the self-supervised credit signal stack. RePro (0.75) finds the asymmetry that online progress prompting hurts while retrospective assessment helps — this empirical constraint is important for future agent RL design.

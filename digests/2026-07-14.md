SVR-R1 (0.88) is today's most significant paper. It applies the PAG/Tango verifier co-training paradigm (vault Jul 13) directly to multimodal models: the VLM is both policy and verifier, issuing binary self-verdicts and triggering rethinks on 'No'. The decreasing-reliance training dynamic is the VLM counterpart of Tango's co-evolution insight — as RL progresses, the policy internalizes self-correction, narrowing the gap between verification and generation capability. This closes the open question of whether the verifier co-training thread extends to multimodal reasoning: it does, and without any external critic.

KV-PRM (0.85) resolves a practical bottleneck that has been implicit in the vault's PRM thread (GroundedPRM, HRM, RL^V). Text-based PRMs score at O(L²) due to trajectory re-encoding — prohibitive in long multi-agent rollouts. KV-PRM's key insight is that the KV cache produced during generation is not just a memory optimization but a richer information carrier than text, enabling O(L) scoring via a single verify token. The 5,000x FLOPs reduction makes PRMs practical for the multi-agent TTS settings that RL^V, T1, and Video-RTS are designed for.

RSPO (0.82) connects to the multi-turn agent credit assignment thread (Turn-Level Reward, HRM). The reward-swap insight resolves a tension the vault has tracked: dense process rewards improve convergence speed but misalign the optimization objective. RSPO uses dense rewards only for trajectory *sampling* — not for *optimization* — maintaining outcome-reward integrity while benefiting from richer exploration signals. The WebShop/ALFWorld evaluation extends the reward design thread beyond math to practical web-shopping and household task agents.

ARMOR (0.80) and DISA (0.78) address training stability from complementary angles. ARMOR shows that reverse KL fails coverage in the over-optimization regime and introduces off-policy anchor samples to prevent policy escape from the reference support. DISA shows that online partition function learning in distribution-matching RL introduces calibration errors that distort gradients, and moves this estimation offline. Together they characterize two failure modes in GRPO-based training: (1) policy over-optimization beyond reference support (ARMOR), (2) partition function miscalibration collapsing diversity (DISA).

**Emerging framework — multi-turn agent reward design (updated):** The vault now has: HRM (hierarchical consecutive-step reward for math), Turn-Level Reward (per-turn verification signal for search agents), RSPO (reward-swap: dense for sampling, outcome for optimization, WebShop/ALFWorld), and DART (tree-based process advantage for agentic tool use). The core tension across all four is dense-vs-sparse credit assignment; RSPO is the first to explicitly separate the two roles by function (exploration vs. optimization).

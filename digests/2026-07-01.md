PASS (0.84) is the structural standout: it closes the gap between the rubric-reward pipeline (ARES/ARBOR/EvoRubrics) and the GRPO algorithm by naming three failure modes that arise regardless of the process signal source. The three pathologies — channel contamination, resolution mismatch, cumulative trap — are architectural rather than hyperparameter problems, which explains why simply concatenating PRM rewards with GRPO has underperformed expectations across the literature.

Pessimism's Paradox (0.82) is the most counterintuitive result: the standard recipe of using conservative DPO initialization as a "safe" foundation for online RL makes things worse, not better. The causal chain (entropy compression → distribution concentration → higher epistemic uncertainty exploitable during adaptation) means the very properties that make DPO conservative offline make it more vulnerable online. The β* calibration result provides an actionable fix.

FlowTracer (0.82) completes the credit assignment hierarchy with an attention-based DAG approach that is orthogonal to the rollout state graph (GraphGPO, Jun 30) and the n-gram contrast (STRIDE, Jun 20). The hierarchy now has five structural axes: rollout state graph (GraphGPO), attention DAG (FlowTracer), sibling-step (SCPO), trajectory-segment (DRE), and token-level (AT-RL). FlowTracer is also the closest mechanistic bridge to the ReasonMaxxer/SWITCH finding — attention hubs in FlowTracer's DAG correspond exactly to the high-entropy commitment positions identified as RL intervention sites.

ACPO (0.80) provides the theoretical foundation explaining why the proliferating GRPO variants (DAPO, CISPO, DEEP-GRPO, EP-GRPO, etc.) each win on different benchmarks despite contradicting each other: off-policy degree controls which tokens are effectively clipped, and clipping controls which tokens learn. Per-group adaptive clipping (ACPO) addresses this directly. Combined with PASS, today's digest provides a nearly complete mechanistic picture of GRPO failure modes at both the signal input layer (PASS) and the optimization update layer (ACPO).

Why RL Generalizes Better (0.80) resolves a puzzle about the interest profile's VLM dimension: RL's generalization advantage on VLMs is not algorithmic but data-selective — it acts as an implicit curriculum that avoids hard samples. DC-SFT replicating this via explicit filtering suggests that RL-distilled data pipelines (queued Jun 30 as thread 4) may be replaceable with cheaper offline curation, which has major practical implications for the multimodal training thread.

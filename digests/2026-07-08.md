Self-Improvement Can Self-Regress (0.88) is the most significant paper today. It adds a fifth failure mode to the vault's RLVR taxonomy: within-task rise-and-collapse, where policy over-optimization on a fixed distribution causes within-campaign collapse independent of cross-task forgetting. This mode operates at a different temporal scale than the other four (campaign-level vs. within-run level) and requires different remedies (peak detection via ES, not regularization). The finding that GRPO raises the floor but does not eliminate the cliff is counterintuitive given GRPO's reputation as a robust RLVR algorithm, and it directly challenges the assumption that group-relative normalization prevents over-optimization.

GRPO-λ (0.85) closes the gap between MRPO's manual step-position schedule and learned credit assignment: eligibility traces from TD(λ) theory provide a principled, data-driven approximation of token-level credit that outperforms GRPO by 3–4.5 points on math reasoning benchmarks. The critic-free implementation is important for practical adoption — it integrates into existing GRPO pipelines without adding a value model. Together with MRPO (manual schedule) and EGSPO (entropy gating), the vault now has three complementary approaches to token-level credit assignment in GRPO: temporal discount (MRPO), TD-theoretic eligibility (GRPO-λ), and entropy-gated routing (EGSPO).

EGSPO (0.78) connects the token-level credit thread to the hybrid SFT+RL literature: by routing token-level gradients based on entropy rather than applying a uniform PPO update, it bridges the sample-level SFT/RL mixing of CHORD with the token-level precision of GRPO-λ. The dual-branch advantage preservation (attenuated but non-zero for low-entropy tokens) is the key design choice that prevents reinforcement of confident wrong predictions — directly addressing the SeL failure mode at the token level.

Reaching Beyond the Mode (0.75) adds a sixth candidate failure mode to the RLVR taxonomy: answer-distribution collapse, where standard RL collapses the model's implicit distribution over valid answers. Multi-answer RL is a principled remedy that internalizes best-of-k diversity into the model's generative objective. This is structurally the output-side complement to TA-GRPO's input-side diversity intervention.

TEMPO (0.72) validates mode-separated GRPO as an architectural primitive in a third domain (after AutoTool for tool invocation and RL-PLUS for on/off-policy mixing), and introduces the hard-prerequisite phase gate as a new variant of mode separation — stricter than concurrent dual-mode (AutoTool) or interpolated hybrid (RL-PLUS).

**Emerging framework — RLVR failure mode taxonomy update:** The vault now has six candidate failure modes: (1) information self-locking (SeL), (2) diversity/gradient collapse (TA-GRPO), (3) capability boundary collapse (RL-PLUS), (4) view-dependent advantage collapse (ConsistRoll), (5) rise-and-collapse / within-task over-optimization (Self-Regress), (6) answer-distribution collapse (Reaching Beyond the Mode). Modes 1–4 operate within a single training run; mode 5 operates across training campaigns; mode 6 operates at the output distribution level. No paper in the vault yet addresses the interaction between these six modes or proposes a unified remedy.

**Emerging framework — token-level credit assignment:** Three papers now form a complementary triad: MRPO (manual temporal discount), GRPO-λ (TD-theoretic eligibility traces), EGSPO (entropy-gated routing). A natural synthesis would combine eligibility traces (for temporal precision) with entropy gating (for exploration vs. exploitation routing) in a single training update — neither paper proposes this combination.

**Emerging framework — mode-separated RL variants:** TEMPO's phase-gated sequencing adds a third implementation variant to the mode-switching primitive: AutoTool (concurrent dual-mode exploration), RL-PLUS (interpolated hybrid policy), TEMPO (sequential phase gate with hard prerequisite). The phase gate is the most conservative (never trades compliance for performance) and the most constrainable (formal monotonic guarantee), suggesting it may be more appropriate for safety-critical constraints while concurrent dual-mode is better for efficiency-correctness tradeoffs.

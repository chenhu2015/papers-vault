The multimodal angle (search 4) confirmed that VLM reward design is an active but underexplored-in-vault direction.

Aha Moment Revisited (0.88) closes the open question from Jul 14 about SVR-R1's test-time behavior, but with a discouraging answer: the verifier co-training thread that is closed for text LLMs (Tango, PAG, RL^V) does not transfer to inference-time VLM gains. SVR-R1 shows training-time benefits from verifier co-training in VLMs; this paper shows those gains do not compound at inference time through self-verification. The asymmetry between text and visual modalities is the key finding: visual information is not effectively integrated into VLM self-verification, so generation diversity (majority voting) dominates over verification quality.

Multimodal Reward Hacking (0.87) provides the diagnostic complement to the reward design thread. The vault has covered reward design (RSPO, ARMOR, DISA) from the perspective of training stability; this paper covers it from the failure mode perspective in the multimodal setting. NRFR is a useful new metric: it distinguishes RL-created failures from SFT-inherited ones, showing that RL can systematically generate new failure modes under outcome-only rewards. The VLM-as-judge semantic verification reducing hacking (vs. keyword-based checks increasing it) directly informs reward design: the quality of the verifier signal under optimization pressure is the decisive variable.

Visually Grounded Self-Reflection (0.83) bridges the gap between Aha Moment Revisited's negative result (VLM self-verification doesn't work at test time) and the training design question: how do you train a VLM that genuinely self-corrects? The answer is trajectory prefix masking + experience replay — both mechanisms force the model to practice recovery from diverse failure states, not just success paths. This is structurally the closest VLM analog to RSPO's training insight: you need to deliberately engineer the training distribution to cover the behaviors you want at inference time.

LLMs as a Jury (0.82) is the most conceptually surprising paper today. The no-cost alternative to PRMs — cross-model consensus — closes the entire gap to an oracle on competition math, outperforming GroundedPRM and KV-PRM without any PRM training. The closed-form predictive law is the most practically useful contribution: it tells you in advance when to trust jury consensus and where it fails (shared-error floor). This doesn't invalidate the PRM thread; rather, it identifies when PRMs add value — specifically at the shared-error floor where independent models converge on wrong answers (science reasoning, not math).

SCI-PRM (0.78) extends the tool-aware PRM thread from math (GroundedPRM) to science, completing a general pattern: tool execution as ground-truth verifier for process reward, applicable across any domain with executable tools. The advantage disappearance mitigation finding also confirms that dense tool-step rewards help RL convergence in the same way RSPO's dense-for-sampling approach does — the key is using dense signals for convergence without corrupting the outcome objective.

**Emerging framework — VLM RL asymmetry (new):** Three papers now characterize a fundamental text/VLM asymmetry in RL training: SVR-R1 (training-time verifier co-training works for VLMs), Aha Moment Revisited (inference-time self-verification doesn't transfer to VLMs), VRRL (training distribution engineering via prefix masking + replay can produce genuine VLM self-reflection). The unifying insight: VLM self-correction requires deliberate training distribution coverage of failure states, not just RL training with a verifier — generation diversity dominates over verification quality at inference time for current VLMs.

TACO (0.86) is today's most significant paper and opens a new sub-dimension within token-level credit assignment. The Positive-Credit Contamination failure mode is conceptually distinct from EGRSD's entropy gating and RL-ZVP's zero-variance recovery: where EGRSD downweights uncertain tokens (those the policy is unsure about) and RL-ZVP upweights uncertain tokens for zero-variance prompts, TACO specifically targets the *positive credit* applied to tokens that are *low-probability given the local context* — i.e., tokens the model finds surprising in context, not just uncertain. A token can be low-entropy (the policy is confident about *something*) yet contextually erroneous (the selected token is implausible given the local generation context). TACO's tail-risk score captures this second type of unreliability. The distinction between "uncertain token" (high policy entropy) and "contextually implausible token" (low p(token | local context)) is the key insight that separates TACO from prior entropy-based approaches.

FaithRL (0.82) closes the text-only gap opened by CORA: faithfulness hallucinations exist in SRMs trained with outcome-based RL, not only in multimodal GRPO. The step-level PRM faithfulness reward is the natural complement to CORA's consistency reward model — both add auxiliary signals that the outcome reward cannot provide, but at different granularities (step-level vs. trace-answer-level). The truncated resampling strategy is a practical contribution to mitigate reward hacking from step-level signals, analogous to how HRAS in CORA prevents the consistency reward from crowding out the task reward.

CoBA-RL (0.78) adds a capability-aware sampling signal to the data efficiency thread. The key distinction from existing approaches: IGRPO operates within a prompt (which branches to expand), RL-ZVP operates on prompt-level variance (recovering zero-variance prompts), and GeoMin/GEOALIGN operate on rollout-level geometric quality. CoBA-RL operates at a higher level — which prompts to sample more from given the model's *current* capability state. The Capability-Oriented Value function tracks training gain dynamically, avoiding the static pass-rate proxy used in most budget allocation methods.

ACRE (0.73) is an Oct 2025 paper included as contextual predecessor to CORA. It quantifies reasoning-answer misalignment (79.7% consistency on MMVU under standard GRPO) and introduces option-shuffling as a consistency signal — a simpler mechanism than CORA's HRAS but with the distinct finding that option-ordering bias is a separate contributing factor. Together ACRE and CORA establish the full picture: reasoning traces decouple from final answers due to (a) option-ordering spurious patterns (ACRE) and (b) semantic inconsistency between thought chain and answer (CORA).

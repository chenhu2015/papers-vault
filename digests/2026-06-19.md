**All 5 papers from queries 2, 3, and 4.** Query 1 (multi-objective Pareto) is confirmed broken — abandon the "Pareto + RL + LLM" formulation. The rubric thread (query 2) was the most productive, yielding 3 of 5 digest papers. The world model query (query 3) was confirmed off-target but surfaced DRE as an outlier catch. The GRPO zero-gradient problem (ARBOR) and sequence-level credit mis-assignment (DRE) are two related but distinct failure modes of GRPO training now both addressed in the vault.

ARBOR (0.87) is the standout: the zero-gradient problem in GRPO (all-correct or all-incorrect groups) has been noted implicitly in prior vault papers (RODS Jun 18 addressed it from the data side), but ARBOR is the first paper to directly name it and solve it via process reward injection. The rubric memory that accumulates and consolidates cross-query rubrics is a form of curriculum memory — analogous in purpose to MemoPilot (Jun 10) and InfoMem (Jun 10), but operating at the reward level rather than the policy level.

DRE (0.81) closes a conceptual gap in the credit assignment thread: AT-RL (Jun 14) handles token-level credit within a step, HiMPO (Jun 18) handles action-level credit for memory writes, and DRE handles prefix/suffix credit within a successful trajectory. Together these three papers define a hierarchy of credit assignment granularity — token (AT-RL), trajectory-segment (DRE), action-type (HiMPO) — all operating on the same fundamental GRPO limitation: the group-relative advantage cannot distinguish quality at finer granularities than the trajectory.

ARES (0.80) completes a rubric-based reward pipeline: ARES generates rubrics offline from pretraining data, ARBOR generates and refines rubrics online during training, RUBAS operationalizes rubrics for the safety-utility tradeoff in tool use. The pipeline shows rubric-based RL as a mature alternative to both RLHF (human labels) and RLVR (verifiable ground truth) for open-ended agent training.
